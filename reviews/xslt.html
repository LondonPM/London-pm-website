XSLT

Author: Doug Tidwell

ISBN:

0596000537

Publisher: O'Reilly

Reviewed by: Dave Hodgkinson

Like many reviews, this is going to be as much about me as it is about the book. I own a web site (plug: http://www.deep-purple.com) with vast amounts of content (5000+ pages) and have had a nagging feeling that the content needed to be more structured. Plus it would be nice to be able to deliver the content to multiple platforms relatively easily.

With my consulting hat on, I had actually recommended XSLT on one occasion where the site designers and coders were from different companies and a very firm divide between code, content and presentation were required.

So I was hoping to grok XSLT.

Coming to XML from a "knowing what it's all about but never actually having had to do anything with it" viewpoint is was very nice to be guided gently through the initial concepts. Then it starts to get nasty, and where it loses me.

XML, I get. Having tight, defined data formats is a good thing. Being able to transform that data for many different media is also a good thing but the hoops you have to jump through to get any results at all seem daunting. I shall stick to the Template Toolkit and wait on Matt Sargeant to produce something pragmatic.

As an O'Reilly, this is as competent as they get. Even if there are 236 pages of the main book and 204 pages of Appendix.

Overall, this was too far removed from anything I know or want to do to be of any use to me.
